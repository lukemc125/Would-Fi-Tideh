Remembering Miss Lou

The Hon. Louise Bennett-Coverley

“Miss Lou” · Pioneering poet, folklorist & educator

The Hon. Louise Bennett-Coverley, affectionately known as “Miss Lou,” was a pioneering Jamaican poet, folklorist, and educator. She is widely recognized as one of the first and most influential figures to legitimize Jamaican Patois (Patwa) as a respected literary craft and a language of profound cultural expression.

At a time when the dialect was often dismissed as “broken English,” Miss Lou celebrated its vivid imagery, humor, and rhythmic cadence. Through her poems, performances, and radio broadcasts, she elevated the voice of the ordinary Jamaican, ensuring that the rich oral traditions, wisdom, and “wuds” of the island were preserved and celebrated on the global stage. This platform is built in the spirit of her legacy.

Honouring Marcus Garvey

The Rt. Excellent Marcus Mosiah Garvey

Jamaica’s first National Hero · Visionary Pan-Africanist

The Rt. Excellent Marcus Mosiah Garvey, Jamaica’s first National Hero, was a visionary Pan-Africanist and a towering figure in the global fight for Black pride, self-reliance, and cultural identity.

While Miss Lou celebrated the unique beauty of Jamaican Patois, Garvey recognized that true emancipation required a deep knowledge of one’s history and the confident use of one’s voice. He famously noted the profound power of language and speech when he said:

“The pen is mightier than the sword, but the tongue is mightier than them both put together.”

This powerful reminder of the weight of our spoken words is the very essence of Wud fi Tideh. Preserving our proverbs is not just about remembering old sayings; it is about keeping the powerful tongue and roots of the Jamaican people alive for generations to come, ensuring we are never, as Garvey warned, “like a tree without roots.”

Remembering Claude McKay

Claude McKay

Poet of the Harlem Renaissance · Pioneer of Patois verse

Born in the hills of Clarendon, Jamaica, Claude McKay carried the voice of the island to the world. In 1912 he published “Songs of Jamaica” and “Constab Ballads,” the first books of poetry ever written in Jamaican Patois, proving that the everyday tongue of his people belonged on the printed page and in the halls of literature.

He went on to become a central voice of the Harlem Renaissance, his defiant sonnet “If We Must Die” (1919) and his celebrated novel “Home to Harlem” (1928) giving proud, unflinching expression to the Black experience. Like Miss Lou and Marcus Garvey, McKay understood that a people’s words are their power — and he wrote in his own, so that those who followed could do the same.
